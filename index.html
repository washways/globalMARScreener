Global Managed Aquifer Recharge Screener

The Global Managed Aquifer Recharge Screener is a Google Earth Engine wrapper for exploring where landscapes may be more suitable for managed aquifer recharge investigation across countries, ADM1 regions, and ADM2 districts.

It combines hydrogeomorphic opportunity, broad physical feasibility, screening-level constraints, and intervention-setting guidance into a single interactive map.

How it works

The app is designed as a transparent GIS screening tool, not a black box. It evaluates where recharge opportunity appears plausible, where the terrain and land surface may be practically feasible, and where constraints or caution flags are stronger.

1

Opportunity

Highlights hydrogeomorphic settings where runoff concentration, valley position, footslope tendency, or drainage adjacency may support recharge interventions.

2

Feasibility

Summarises broad terrain, land-cover, soil-water, and depth-to-bedrock conditions that make practical recharge more or less plausible.

3

Constraint

Flags areas where steep slopes, built-up land, ridges, or excessive wetness may reduce suitability or require added caution.

4

Confidence

Provides a broad indication of where the evidence base is stronger versus where results should be treated more cautiously.

5

Combined suitability

The main display blends the signals above into a relative screening surface to help prioritise where detailed follow-up may be worthwhile.

The model uses transparent expert-judgement defaults and open geospatial datasets. Results are relative to the selected area and should be interpreted locally.

Intervention settings

These are broad hydrogeomorphic typologies to guide next-stage investigation, not final engineering prescriptions.

Riverbank / floodplain

Low-lying, drainage-adjacent zones that may suit riverbank filtration, flood spreading, off-channel basins, or alluvial recharge where local conditions allow.

Tributary / valley

Smaller valleys and tributary corridors that may support check dams, sand dams, recharge weirs, and small valley-spreading approaches.

Footslope / colluvial

Lower-slope transition areas that may be more promising for infiltration trenches, contour bunds, managed runoff capture, and recharge-supporting soil and water conservation.

Upland / distributed

Open, gentle terrain that may be better suited to distributed recharge enhancement and landscape-scale infiltration support rather than large engineered structures.

How to use the app

A typical workflow for planners, hydrogeologists, and GIS teams.

  1. Select a country and, if needed, narrow to ADM1 or ADM2.
  2. Run the current selection to generate the screening layers.
  3. Use the layer selector to switch between suitability, intervention, opportunity, feasibility, constraint, confidence, and diagnostics.
  4. Adjust opacity to compare the model output with the administrative boundary and base map.
  5. Interpret high-signal areas as candidates for follow-up investigation, not as confirmed MAR sites.
National outputs are useful for strategic screening. District-scale interpretation is more helpful for local prioritisation, but still requires ground validation.

Limitations and caveats

Use this tool to prioritise where to look next, not to skip fieldwork.

  • Screening tool only; not a final site-selection model
  • Does not replace field hydrogeology, geophysics, monitoring, or engineering design
  • Relative scores are influenced by local normalisation within the selected area
  • Global datasets cannot fully capture fracture connectivity or aquifer hydraulics
  • Does not assess water quality, legal restrictions, safeguards, or lifecycle cost
  • Some inputs are proxies rather than direct measurements
  • Built-up or flood-prone areas may still require strong caution even when opportunity looks high
  • All outputs should be checked against local data and expertise

Recommended next steps

After screening a country or district, the next step is evidence gathering.

  • Review geology, regolith, and existing groundwater information for the highlighted areas.
  • Cross-check with borehole logs, groundwater levels, and known recharge structures where available.
  • Assess water quality, land tenure, safeguards, access, and flood behavior before investment.
  • Use local hydrogeological expertise to recalibrate or reinterpret the screening outputs where needed.
